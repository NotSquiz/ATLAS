## MidJourney V7 handles subsurface scattering differently than V6

V7 represents a fundamental shift in how MidJourney processes light transport. Where V6 approximated subsurface scattering effects, **V7 simulates them with physical accuracy**—understanding how light bounces through skin, refracts through glass, and creates soft shadows from diffused sources. This means the same prompt in V7 produces more realistic translucency without needing as many modifier terms.

The practical impact: V7 distinguishes between materials like brushed aluminum versus polished chrome, old leather versus new leather. For your clay-with-inner-glow aesthetic, V7 can differentiate between matte clay surfaces and translucent wax-like materials when given the right cues.

**Optimal parameters for translucent clay lit from within:**
```
--v 7 --s 200-300 --chaos 15-20 --q 2
```

The **--stylize** parameter ranges from 0-1000 in V7. For SSS/glow work, stay in the **150-400 range**—below 150 produces too literal an interpretation, while above 500 pushes toward abstract artistic flourishes that may overwhelm the subtle glow effect. The **--chaos** parameter at 10-25 provides useful variation while maintaining control over material consistency.

V7 also introduces the **--exp parameter** (0-100) for advanced tone mapping. Keep this under 25 for prompt consistency—higher values can enhance luminosity but may push glow effects toward overexposure. The **--weird parameter is unavailable in V7**, so avoid including it.

**Key V6 vs V7 difference for your pipeline:** V6 produced what users described as a "waxy quality" in skin—which actually benefits the translucent clay look you want. V7's more natural skin rendering means you may need to explicitly request `wax-like texture` or `soft skinned` to recover that effect.

## Animation tools and the glow preservation challenge

All three major AI video generators—Kling, Runway Gen-3, and Luma Dream Machine—can animate glowing content, but **temporal flickering remains the universal challenge**. Details and textures "shimmer" or change inconsistently between frames, particularly high-contrast luminance transitions like glow effects.

### Kling AI 2.1/2.5: Best temporal stability

Kling explicitly supports terms like "glowing trees, bioluminescent flowers" and "neon glow" with realistic light interaction on surfaces. Its **Motion Brush feature** allows isolating glowing elements for targeted animation, preventing whole-scene instability.

**Known issues:** Uneven color transitions in gradient-heavy visuals, occasional text artifacts, some face deformation in stylized content.

**Recommended Kling prompt for glowing characters:**
```
A weary adventurer enters a misty enchanted forest. Wide shot of massive 
glowing trees, bioluminescent flowers lighting the path. The camera lowers 
to ground level, following boots as fog curls around them. Consistent ethereal 
glow throughout, dreamlike atmosphere, cinematic quality.
```

### Runway Gen-3 Alpha: Official bioluminescent support

Runway's prompting guide explicitly includes this example:
```
A glowing ocean at night time with bioluminescent creatures under water. 
The camera starts with a macro close-up of a glowing jellyfish and then 
expands to reveal the entire ocean lit up with various glowing colors 
under a starry sky.
```

Gen-3's "advanced temporal modeling" solves previous frame jitter issues—characters, lighting, and perspective remain stable throughout clips. However, **for videos over 10 seconds, inconsistencies in object shape, position, or appearance can emerge**.

### Luma Ray3: Native HDR wins for glow fidelity

Luma's Ray3 model is the world's first AI video generator with **native 16-bit High Dynamic Range output**—critical for preserving bright, glowing elements without clipping or degradation. The EXR export (10-bit, 12-bit, 16-bit options) enables professional color grading workflows.

**Luma prompt structure:**
```
Main subject → Action → Details → Scene → Style → Camera → Reinforcer
```

Include lighting cues like "sunset glow," "soft glow reflects in her eyes," or "luminescent mushrooms."

### Comparative flicker resistance (ranked best to worst)

1. **Veo 3** — Best overall consistency
2. **Kling 2.1 Pro** — Excellent temporal stability, minimizes artifacts
3. **Kling 2.5 Turbo** — Fast with good stability
4. **Runway Gen 4** — Good but adds slight camera movement
5. **Luma Ray 2** — Less stable for static scenes; Ray3 improves this

### Mitigation strategies for glow flickering

- Simplify visual complexity—reduce the number of detailed glowing elements
- Include explicit consistency prompts: `"consistent appearance throughout the video"` and `"distinctive features remain unchanged"`
- Add 1-second frame padding at video edges
- Consider post-processing with tools like Higgsfield's Sora 2 Enhancer to reduce flickering

## Reference art direction: Pixar leads the way

### Pixar's *Soul* (2020) — The gold standard for volumetric ethereal characters

The soul characters are "conceptual beings of light" with a prism-like response to lighting. Rather than hard surfaces with subsurface scattering, they appear translucent with internal luminescence. Pixar developed a custom **"Iridescence Driver" (IDriver)** shading node that feeds into a Double Henyey-Greenstein volume shader.

The IDriver determines albedo color by using normals to look up into a color ramp, faking diffuse and specular lobes. "Soft normals" filtering removes smaller details outside the face, creating the extra-soft appearance while maintaining readable expressions—critical for children's content.

### Pixar's *Elemental* (2023) — Emotion-linked luminescence

Ember's character demonstrates how internal glow can reflect emotional states: fire becomes "more transparent and candle-like" when vulnerable, shifts to purple when angry. The fire characteristics map to emotional controls ("zero to 10 sadness control"). This approach—**translucency and glow mapped to narrative/emotional beats**—could inform your "glowing translucent (unconscious) to structured wood (conscious)" transition.

### Foxfire aesthetic references

Foxfire—bioluminescent fungi on decaying wood—produces a soft bluish-green glow historically called "fairy fire." Artist **Marta Djourina's 2021 "Foxfire" series** used analogue photography with cultivated bioluminescent mushrooms, creating ethereal magenta-on-paper images. The natural blue-green glow from luciferin/luciferase reactions provides the specific organic quality you're targeting—soft, diffuse, atmospheric rather than harsh or artificial.

Visual characteristics to aim for:
- Blue-green to yellow-green glow range
- Warm wood base tones contrasting with cool bioluminescent accents
- Soft, diffuse light (no sharp edges)
- Atmospheric, not spotlit
